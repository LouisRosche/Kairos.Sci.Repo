## Executive Summary

This curriculum design implements **strategic interleaving** (Rohrer, 2012) to remediate Cycle 2 misconceptions while advancing pacing. Rather than explicit re-teaching, Cycle 3 content creates contexts where:
1. Incorrect mental models generate visible cognitive dissonance
2. Successful task completion requires retrieval of Cycle 2 concepts
3. Transfer is assessed through novel phenomena, not repeated prompts

**Research Foundation:**
- Rohrer (2012): Interleaving forces discrimination between similar concepts
- Roediger & Karpicke (2006): Retrieval practice > re-study for retention
- Smithsonian Science (2024): Three-dimensional assessment requires integrated SEP-DCI-CCC tasks

**Phenomenon:** "Why does a car get hot inside but not outside?"

**NGSS Performance Expectation:** MS-ESS3-5
> Ask questions to clarify evidence of the factors that have caused the rise in global temperatures over the past century.

**3D Integration:**
- **DCI:** ESS3.D (Human impacts on Earth systems)
- **SEP:** Asking Questions and Defining Problems
- **CCC:** Cause and Effect; Energy and Matter

#### Station 1: Molecular Vibration & Bond Energy (INTERLEAVED RETRIEVAL)
**Spiral Target:** Students MUST correctly apply bond energy concepts to explain IR absorption
**Resource:** PhET "Molecules and Light" simulation (https://phet.colorado.edu/en/simulations/molecules-and-light)

**Task:** Analyze PhET simulation showing CO‚ÇÇ molecules absorbing infrared radiation
- WHY does CO‚ÇÇ absorb IR but not N‚ÇÇ?
- Draw molecular model showing energy transfer
- **CRITICAL:** "When IR energy is absorbed, do the bonds in CO‚ÇÇ break or just vibrate faster?"

**Misconception Trap:** Students who believe "breaking bonds releases energy" will incorrectly predict CO‚ÇÇ breaks apart. The simulation shows molecules vibrating, NOT breaking‚Äîforcing conceptual revision.

**Success Criteria:**
1. Correctly identify that bonds ABSORB energy to vibrate
2. Explain that breaking would REQUIRE more energy than IR provides
3. Connect to Cycle 2: Endothermic processes absorb energy

#### Station 2: Carbon Cycle Conservation (INTERLEAVED RETRIEVAL)
**Spiral Target:** Conservation of mass in Earth systems

**Task:** Trace carbon atoms through photosynthesis ‚Üí respiration ‚Üí combustion ‚Üí atmosphere
- Use manipulatives to physically move "carbon atoms" through cycle
- Balance each transformation (atoms in = atoms out)
- Calculate: If 1 tree absorbs 48 lbs CO‚ÇÇ/year, how many carbon atoms is that?

**Misconception Trap:** Students who believe mass disappears will struggle to balance the cycle. Physical atom manipulatives make conservation visible.

**Success Criteria:**
1. Demonstrate conservation through all cycle steps
2. Connect to Cycle 2: Chemical reactions rearrange, don't destroy atoms
3. Quantify carbon storage in different reservoirs

#### Station 3: Engineering Challenge‚ÄîDesign a Thermal Trap
**Spiral Target:** Apply thermal energy concepts to solve problems

**Task:** Design and model a structure that maximizes heat retention
- Given constraints: 3 materials, limited surface area
- Must explain WHY each material choice affects thermal energy flow
- **INTERLEAVED:** Connect to Cycle 2 energy concepts‚Äîwhich materials absorb more thermal energy? Which conduct heat faster?

**Success Criteria:**
1. Evidence-based material selection using thermal properties
2. Correct explanation of heat trapping mechanism (absorption, conduction, radiation)
3. Connection to molecular-level energy storage (particles moving faster = higher temperature)

## Grade 8: Natural Selection & Forces in Nature
### MS-LS4-4, MS-LS4-6 + Spiral to MS-PS2-1, MS-PS2-2

### Week 1: The Cheetah-Gazelle Arms Race
**Phenomenon:** "If cheetahs are the fastest land animal, why haven't gazelles gone extinct?"

**NGSS Performance Expectation:** MS-LS4-4
> Construct an explanation based on evidence that describes how genetic variations of traits in a population increase some individuals' probability of surviving and reproducing in a specific environment.

**3D Integration:**
- **DCI:** LS4.B (Natural Selection)
- **SEP:** Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions
- **CCC:** Cause and Effect; Stability and Change

#### Station 1: Predator-Prey Force Analysis (INTERLEAVED RETRIEVAL)
**Spiral Target:** Newton's Third Law in biological systems

**Task:** Analyze slow-motion video of cheetah-gazelle chase, focusing on the moment of contact/collision
- Identify action-reaction force pairs when cheetah strikes gazelle
- Calculate relative accelerations using F=ma during collision
- **CRITICAL QUESTION:** "When the cheetah's body contacts the gazelle's body, does the cheetah exert more force on the gazelle, or do they exert equal forces on each other?"

**Misconception Trap:** Students who believe "larger animals exert more force" will predict the cheetah exerts greater force. Newton's Third Law requires equal and opposite forces during ANY contact‚Äîthe 50kg cheetah and 25kg gazelle exert EQUAL forces on each other, but experience DIFFERENT accelerations.

**Data Analysis (Collision Scenario):**
- During a 0.2-second collision, cheetah (50 kg) and gazelle (25 kg) exert forces on each other
- Measured: Gazelle accelerates at 20 m/s¬≤ away from cheetah
- Calculate force on gazelle: F = ma = 25 kg √ó 20 m/s¬≤ = 500 N
- **KEY:** If forces are equal (Newton's Third Law), what force does the gazelle exert ON the cheetah? (Answer: 500 N)
- What is the cheetah's acceleration? a = F/m = 500 N / 50 kg = 10 m/s¬≤

**Success Criteria:**
1. Correctly identify Newton's Third Law in predation
2. Calculate accelerations showing smaller mass = greater acceleration
3. Explain evolutionary advantage of lower mass (evasion)

#### Station 2: Trait Variation Simulation
**Task:** Use bean simulation to model trait distribution in population
- Different colored beans represent speed variants
- "Hunt" by grabbing beans with timed constraint
- Graph survival rates by trait over "generations"
- **INTERLEAVED:** Explain selection pressure as a "force" (conceptual analogy)

**Success Criteria:**
1. Demonstrate how variation leads to differential survival
2. Calculate change in trait frequency over generations
3. Connect to forces: Selection pressure pushes population toward certain traits

#### Station 3: Engineering Challenge‚ÄîDesign a Survivor
**Spiral Target:** Apply force concepts to biological design

**Task:** Design an organism to survive a specific predation pressure
- Given constraints: mass budget, energy budget, habitat parameters
- Must justify design using force relationships (F=ma, momentum)
- **INTERLEAVED:** If your organism is smaller, how must it accelerate to escape? Show calculation.

**Success Criteria:**
1. Evidence-based design choices
2. Correct force/acceleration calculations
3. Clear connection between physics principles and survival advantage

## Implementation Notes

### Teacher Facilitation During Stations
- Circulate with clipboard using observation protocol
- Ask probing questions when misconceptions appear
- Flag students for 1:1 follow-up if needed
- Use "strategic pausing" to address whole-class patterns

### Chromebook Management
- All materials accessible via Canvas modules
- Offline-capable Google Forms
- Station rotation timer displayed on board
- Emergency paper backups available

### Pomodoro Integration
- 25-minute focused station work
- 5-minute movement/social break between stations
- Clear visual timer
- Break activities: stretch, quick walk, drink water

### Behavioral Support
- Pre-planned "like-pairs" for collaboration
- Alternative workspace options for regulation needs
- Exit tickets as calm-down activity option
- Positive framing: "Show what you know"

### Accessibility & Accommodations (IEP/504/ELL)

**Universal Design Features (Built into all materials):**
- Chunked instructions (numbered steps, one action per line)
- Visual supports for all abstract concepts (diagrams, manipulatives)
- Multiple means of expression (verbal, written, drawn, recorded)
- Extended time built into asynchronous model

**Tiered Accommodations:**

| Accommodation | Implementation |
|--------------|----------------|
| **Extended time** | Async model allows unlimited time; no time-based penalties |
| **Reduced questions** | Mark "required" questions; others optional but encouraged |
| **Text-to-speech** | Google Forms compatible with screen readers; add audio directions |
| **Simplified language** | Provide vocabulary glossary QR code at each station |
| **Graphic organizers** | Include blank cause-effect diagrams, calculation templates |
| **Frequent breaks** | Pomodoro structure (25 on/5 off) aligns with attention needs |
| **Preferential seating** | Station rotation allows teacher to position strategically |
| **Scribe/dictation** | Voice typing enabled on all Chromebooks |
| **Calculator use** | Permitted for all calculation questions |
| **Word bank** | Provided for short-answer questions requiring vocabulary |

**ELL Supports:**
- Bilingual glossary for key terms (Spanish available)
- Sentence frames for constructed responses: "The evidence shows... because..."
- Partner reading protocol for dense text
- Visual-heavy phenomena (videos, simulations over text)

**Gifted/Advanced Extensions:**
- Challenge problems marked with ‚≠ê in forms
- "Design your own phenomenon" option in Week 3
- Peer teaching opportunities
- Connection to current research articles
