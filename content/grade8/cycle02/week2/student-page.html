ğŸ“š Station 1 Vocabulary (Click to expand)

Kinetic Energy (KE)

The energy an object has because it's moving. Formula: KE = ½mv²

Example: A bowling ball rolling has kinetic energy

Potential Energy (PE)

Stored energy from position or condition that can become kinetic energy

Example: At the top of a hill, the skater has maximum PE

Thermal Energy

Heat energy. KE often transforms into thermal energy during friction

Example: Brake pads get hot from friction

Energy Conservation

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. Total energy stays constant

Example: PE and KE trade back and forth, but total energy stays same

Energy Transformation

When energy changes from one form to another (PE â†’ KE â†’ thermal)

Example: Skater at top (PE) drops down (KE)

Velocity (v)

Speed in a specific direction, measured in m/s

Example: Car moving 20 m/s north

Mass (m)

Amount of matter in an object, measured in kg

Example: Bowling ball has more mass than basketball

Joule (J)

Unit for measuring energy

Example: 50,000 J or 50 kJ

ğŸ’¡ Solving the Paradox

Remember the anchor phenomenon from the beginning? Now you can explain it!

The Crash Test Paradox Explained:

Claim: Crumple zones protect passengers better than rigid bumpers because they extend collision time and transform kinetic energy into deformation.

Evidence: When a car hits a barrier, it has kinetic energy that has to go somewhere. A rigid car stops quickly (short time), while a crumple zone car deforms over a longer time.

Reasoning: By extending the collision time, crumple zones reduce the force on passengers (F = Δp/Δt). The "missing" energy transforms into deformation, heat, and sound instead of injuring passengers. This is energy conservation in actionâ€”energy can't disappear, but we can control where it goes!
