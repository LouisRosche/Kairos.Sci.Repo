COMMON MISTAKE ALERT: "Magnets Have Unlimited Strength"

WRONG: "A magnet can attract from any distance if it's strong enough."
RIGHT: "Magnetic field strength decreases rapidly with distance. Even strong magnets only work at short ranges."
KEY: The force follows an INVERSE relationship - small distance increases have BIG effects on force!

The Phenomenon: The Invisible Force Mystery

Hold a magnet above a table. Slowly move another magnet underneath...

  • At some point, the top magnet jumps or spins
  • The force acts right through solid wood!
  • But why do some magnets attract from inches away while others only work when touching?

And why does distance matter SO much?

Driving Question: Why do some magnets attract through tables while others don't?

Sample Force-Distance Data

Distance (cm)Force (N)Notes
0.54.0Closest position
1.01.0Doubled distance

THE PATTERN: When distance doubles, force drops by ~4x (not 2x)! This is an INVERSE relationship.

HOME ALTERNATIVE: Use this reference data!

Key pattern to notice:

  • At 0.5 cm: Force = 4.0 N
  • At 1.0 cm: Force = 1.0 N (distance x2, force /4)
  • At 2.0 cm: Force = 0.25 N (distance x2 again, force /4 again)
  • This is called an inverse relationship - force drops FASTER than distance increases!
Need extra support? Click here for calculation hints

Understanding the Pattern:

  • 1.0 N -> 0.25 N = divided by 4
  • If distance doubles (1 -> 2 cm), force divides by ~4
  • To predict at 16 cm: take 8 cm value and divide by 4

Sentence Starters:

  • "When distance doubles, the force ___..."
  • "The graph would show a ___ shape because..."
  • "Refrigerator magnets only work close up because..."

Station 3 - Design a Magnetic Levitation Device

25 Points | ~20 Minutes (Highest Value!)

Engineering Challenge: Magnetic Levitation

DESIGN REQUIREMENTS:

  1. Object must levitate at least 1 cm above the base
  2. System must be stable (not flip over or fall)
  3. Must use magnetic repulsion (like poles facing)
  4. Must account for the force-distance relationship

THE STABILITY PROBLEM (Earnshaw's Theorem):

In 1842, scientist Samuel Earnshaw proved that static magnets alone CANNOT create stable levitation! The object will always flip or slide sideways. Real levitation needs:

  • Spinning (gyroscopic stability)
  • Diamagnetic materials (like pyrolytic graphite)
  • Active feedback (electromagnets that adjust constantly)

Real-World Connection: Maglev Trains

Maglev (magnetic levitation) trains use electromagnets with computer-controlled feedback systems to constantly adjust the magnetic force and keep the train stable. They can reach speeds over 370 mph!

Need extra support? Click here for design hints

Design Strategy:

  • For levitation: N facing N OR S facing S (repulsion)
  • For stability: Add guide rails or walls to prevent sliding
  • The levitation height depends on balancing magnetic force with gravity

Sentence Starters:

  • "My design uses ___ poles facing ___ to create repulsion..."
  • "To prevent flipping, I would add..."
  • "The levitation height would be about ___ because..."
