Hook: The Fading Glow Mystery

12 points | ~10 minutes
What to Think About: When you crack a glow stick, you mix two chemicals that react and release energy as light. But why does the glow eventually fade? What's being "used up" inside?

Key Observations

  • Fresh glow stick: Instant bright glow when cracked
  • After several hours: Glow becomes dimmer
  • After a day or more: Completely dark
  • Cannot be "recharged" - unlike a battery!

Station 1: Reaction Evidence Lab

20 points | ~18 minutes
Focus: Learn to identify the five types of evidence that indicate a chemical reaction has occurred.

Five Types of Reaction Evidence

Evidence Type What to Look For Example
Color Change New color appears that wasn't in either reactant pH indicator turning pink
Gas Production Bubbles, fizzing, or foam Baking soda + vinegar
Temperature Change Gets hot (exothermic) or cold (endothermic) Hand warmer heating up
Precipitate Solid appears from mixing liquids White solid from clear solutions
Light Production Glowing without being heated Glow stick (chemiluminescence)
Common Misconception: Not all bubbles indicate a chemical reaction! Bubbles from boiling water or air escaping a liquid are physical changes. Chemical reaction bubbles continue as long as reactants remain.

Station 2: Mass Conservation Investigation

20 points | ~15 minutes
Key Principle: Law of Conservation of Mass - In a chemical reaction, the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. Atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed!

Understanding Mass Conservation

System Type What Happens Mass Observation
Sealed Container Nothing can enter or leave Mass stays exactly the same
Open Container Gas can escape to the air Mass appears to decrease

Why Does Burning Wood Seem to "Lose" Mass?

When wood burns, most of it combines with oxygen and escapes as gases (CO‚ÇÇ and H‚ÇÇO vapor). The ash is just the minerals that couldn't burn. If you could capture ALL the gases, total mass would be conserved!

Common Misconception: Mass is NOT converted to energy in chemical reactions. Energy is released from chemical bonds, but all the atoms (and their mass) remain.

Station 3: Design a Reaction Detector

25 points | ~20 minutes
Design Challenge: A food company needs to detect when milk has spoiled. Spoilage is a chemical reaction that produces lactic acid. Design a detection system!

The Science Behind Spoilage

  • What happens: Bacteria convert lactose (milk sugar) ‚Üí lactic acid
  • Evidence: pH drops from ~6.6 (fresh) to ~4.5 (spoiled)
  • Your tool: pH indicators change color at specific pH values

Design Requirements

  1. Must use at least one type of reaction evidence
  2. Must be food-safe (no contamination)
  3. Must give a clear visual signal
  4. Should work over the product's shelf life
Real-World Connection: Smart food packaging already uses similar chemistry! Some labels contain pH-sensitive dyes that change color when food spoils.

Key Vocabulary

Chemical Reaction
Process where substances combine or break apart to form new substances
Reactant
Starting substance in a chemical reaction
Product
New substance formed by a chemical reaction
Exothermic
Reaction that releases heat (feels warm)
Endothermic
Reaction that absorbs heat (feels cold)
Precipitate
Solid that forms from mixing liquids
Conservation of Mass
Mass before reaction = mass after reaction
Chemiluminescence
Light produced by a chemical reaction
