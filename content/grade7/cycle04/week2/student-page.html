This Week's Mystery

What happens when nutrients run off into waterways? In 2014, a massive algae bloom turned Lake Erie bright green. 500,000 people couldn't drink their tap water. Fish died by the thousands. How did fertilizer meant to help crops end up poisoning a lake?

By the End of This Week, You Will Be Able To:

  1. Trace nitrogen and phosphorus through ecosystems
  2. Explain the eutrophication cascade (nutrients → algae → decomposition → oxygen depletion)
  3. Analyze real-world data from the Gulf of Mexico dead zone
  4. Design a solution to minimize nutrient runoff

Connecting to Previous Learning

Week 1: You learned how CO₂ dissolves in the ocean to cause acidification. This week, you'll see how a DIFFERENT chemical (nitrogen/phosphorus) causes a DIFFERENT problem (eutrophication) through a similar cascade process.

Cycle 3: Remember feedback loops? Eutrophication is another example of POSITIVE feedback - the problem makes itself worse!

Key Vocabulary

Eutrophication
Process where excess nutrients cause explosive algae growth, leading to oxygen depletion
Dead Zone
Area where oxygen levels are too low for most marine life to survive (hypoxia)
Biogeochemical Cycle
Movement of elements (like N, P, C) through living organisms and the physical environment
Nutrient Runoff
Fertilizers, sewage, or organic waste washing into waterways from rain
Algae Bloom
Rapid, explosive growth of algae when nutrients are abundant
Decomposition
Bacteria breaking down dead organic matter, consuming oxygen in the process
