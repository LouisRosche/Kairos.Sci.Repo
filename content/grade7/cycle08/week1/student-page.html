üê∫ The Phenomenon: Wolves Changed Rivers

When wolves were hunted to extinction in Yellowstone by 1926, something unexpected happened over the following decades:

  • Elk populations exploded without wolf predation
  • Riverbanks were overgrazed as elk ate all the willows
  • Rivers widened and meandered without plant roots to stabilize banks
  • Beavers disappeared because they had no willows for food or dam-building

Then in 1995, 14 wolves were reintroduced. Within 20 years, the rivers literally changed course!

ü§î Driving Question: Why did removing wolves from Yellowstone change the rivers?

üìñ Key Vocabulary This Week

TermDefinition
Trophic LevelA position in a food chain (producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, apex predator)
Trophic CascadeWhen changes at one trophic level ripple through and affect all other levels in the ecosystem
Keystone SpeciesA species that has disproportionately large effects on its ecosystem compared to its population size
10% RuleOnly about 10% of energy transfers from one trophic level to the next
Apex PredatorA predator at the top of a food chain that has no natural predators

The 10% Energy Rule

Only about 10% of energy transfers from one trophic level to the next. That's why apex predators are always rare - there's very little energy left at the top of the food chain!

üî¨ Engineering Challenge: Restore an Ecosystem

THE SCENARIO:

A local prairie ecosystem has lost its keystone species (prairie dogs) due to disease. You are a conservation scientist tasked with designing a restoration plan.

  • Prairie dogs burrow and aerate soil
  • Their burrows provide homes for burrowing owls
  • Hawks and coyotes depend on prairie dogs for food
  • Without prairie dogs, vegetation is overgrown and soil is compacting

CONSTRAINTS:

  • Budget: $100,000 total
  • Timeline: 5 years
  • Requirement: Must address at least 3 affected species
